Attendance over time

Every activity (an individual lesson or social) we hold a record for is one point below: its date against the number of people. This is a high-detail view intended for data access. Pinch or scroll to zoom and drag to pan the date axis, double-click to reset; hover or tap a point for a summary, and click a point to pin its full breakdown beneath the chart. Once you enter the PII passphrase in the box above, the breakdown offers a download of that activity's complete record as a CSV — named attendees with their first and last names, anonymous head-counts, and the parent event details; without the passphrase the breakdown still shows but the download is disabled. Note that this won't include committee members or anyone volunteering on the night: they don't buy tickets, so they don't appear in our attendance records.

Reading the chart. Colour is the event type (course, social, workshop, weekender). Marker shape is the difficulty (Level 1/2/3, beginners, intermediate, advanced; a cross for socials, a circle where the level is unknown). Filled markers are confirmed attendance (named attendees marked present plus anonymous door head-counts); hollow markers are everyone registered, whether or not we know they turned up, plus the event's waitlist — people who wanted a place but it was full — so the hollow marker reads as total demand.

How well attended are our Level 1 classes?

Mean Level 1 attendance per lesson, one line per academic year, against the term's slot in the year. Comparing the same term across years shows whether the beginner intake is growing or shrinking. Solid is attended (named attendees plus anonymous door head-counts); dashed is everyone registered, whether or not we know they turned up, plus the course's waitlist — people who wanted a place but it was full — so the gap above the solid line is no-shows plus unmet demand. How well attended are our Level 2 classes and how popular are social-only tickets?

Mean Level 2 attendance per term (solid), one line per academic year, with the social-only ticket purchases overlaid (dashed). Social-only tickets were introduced in 2024/25.

Once someone joins, how long do they keep coming back?

Each row is the term a dancer first appeared; each column is the number of terms later; the colour is the share of that joining cohort still active then. Reading down a column shows whether newer intakes retain better than older ones; reading across a row shows the shape of a cohort's drop-off after joining. Each joining term's row label is tinted by its teaching team, so intakes that shared a team stand out at a glance; hover or tap a cell to see that team by name, and read retention against who taught that intake. Presence here means appearing on any sheet that term, attended or not, because turnout was only recorded consistently from around 2024. The teaching team shows as id codes by default; enter the PII passphrase in the box at the top of the page to relabel them with the teachers' first names.

How big is our community, and how committed?

For everyone who turned up at least once in 2026, this counts the unique dates they came to as a share of the whole 2026 calendar, then plots how many dancers attended at least that share. The leftmost point is the maximum size of the community (everyone who came at all); how steeply the line falls is its commitment - whether attendance concentrates in a regular core or spreads across many occasional visitors. The two lines show the effect of the 30th anniversary, which drew a crowd who largely don't appear otherwise, so the lower line, with it removed, is the underlying regular community. We show 2026 only because that is when we began recording Level 2 attendees by name; before then the Level 2 class attendee names were not recorded, so earlier years would undercount the committed core. Frequently asked questions

What's the difference between "registered" and "attended"?

Registered counts everyone named on the sheet for that activity — whether or not we know they turned up — and, on the hollow markers, adds the event's waitlist: people who wanted a place but it was full, so the hollow figure reads as total demand. Attended counts only people we have a positive record were present, plus anonymous head-counts (see below). They are different measures, so the two markers for one night rarely line up exactly.

What is an anonymous head-count?

Most points combine named records (one pseudonym per person) with aggregate head-counts: anonymous people in the room — typically door or cash sales taken without recording a name. The attended (filled) figure is named attendees plus these aggregate heads; the registered (hollow) figure is named registrations plus the event's waitlist.

How can a night show more attended than registered?

"Registered" only counts people for whom we have a name. We have sometimes sold tickets on the door without recording names, and those door sales show up in the attended head-count without a matching registration.

Are these the true numbers in the room?

No — they run low. We don't routinely log volunteer or committee attendance, so the actual number of people present on a given night is typically 5-10 higher than shown.

Why is Level 2 attendance for some terms in 2026 surprisingly high?

Anyone who turned up to even a single night of a Level 2 term in 2026 is counted as registered for the whole term, so the registered figure reflects everyone who passed through (as if they had bought a block and then turned up on only some nights), not a stable weekly cohort.

Why do the 2026 Level 2 registered numbers jump around?

Those sheets record a single ticket category per person per night, and social-only attendees are separated out from the Level 2 count rather than added to it — so the Level 2 line moves as people switch between dancing the lesson and coming for the social only. Note that the "social only" figure is not a separate, sparsely-attended social event: it's the people on a Level 2 class night who came for the social portion of that same evening rather than the lesson, so small social-only numbers sitting alongside a class are expected, not a poorly-attended party.

Why does a weekender show the same attendance on more than one day?

A weekender (and the Stockbridge Swingout) sells one ticket covering classes that run across several days. We treat checking in at any point as attending every class that ticket entitles you to, so a single class track appears on each of its days with the same people. This is a deliberate modelling choice: it keeps weekender figures comparable and reflects that almost nobody is checked in separately after the first day.

Why does the 2023 Christmas Party show only one attendee?

That event survives only as a ticket-booking export, not an attendance register. We have a note of one person paying on the door (counted as attended), but no record of who else turned up, so everyone else's attendance is marked "unknown" rather than present.

Why is there Level 2 registration data for early 2022 and then nothing?

It appears that Level 2 classes were sold as a block in early 2022. The early-2022 course was tracked with a hand-kept class-list register that recorded the full room, including door and cash payers. From spring 2022 onward the only surviving records are dancecloud booking exports, which captured just the people who bought a block ticket online (a handful per course at the time) and then the practice of selling Level 2 blocks appears to have ended. So the drop-off is a change in record-keeping, not a real collapse in attendance.

Why are the 2022–2023 attendance points on the all-activities chart oddly uniform?

Those points aren't single sessions. For terms before we kept per-night records the database holds only enrolment, not turnout, so instead we take each term's mean attendance — recovered from older analysis summaries and the 2023 AGM report — and stamp it on every class night of that term (the nights that term's Level 1 course ran; Level 2 ran alongside it). They're drawn as ordinary course markers (a filled triangle for Level 1, a square for Level 2) so they sit in context, but because every night in a term carries the same term average rather than its real figure, they line up in flat rows unlike the genuine per-session scatter around them.
